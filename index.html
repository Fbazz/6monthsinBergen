Six months in Bergen

An introduction


I have lived in Bergen from January to July 2019. I was working at UiB (Universitetet i Bergen) as a guest researcher, and in my spare time I tried to explore as much as I could. Exploring is a form of learning, after all.

At first it was quite a shock for me, an Italian used to crowds, noises, and home-cooked pasta. Norway was very different from everything I knew, and so were Norwegian people. After a while, though, I guess that it all grew on me. Now that I work and spend my time in a metropolis like Rome, I kinda miss the quiet life in Bergen.With this portfolio, I'd like to share with you guys some of the places and sights I loved the most during my stay. Below, I have also listed some tips you should really be aware of if you are planning a trip to Bergen. I hope it will be helpful. Enjoy! ❤️

The best places in town

Here are my top 6 places in Bergen. I guess I'm not much of a party animal since all of them show the prominent presence of silence . I decided to show you famous places, along with special spots that are less known to tourists. For each of them there is a link to get further details.

Mount Fløyen

It is one of Norway’s most visited attractions. You can reach the top by the city center using the Funicular or just walking from the Fish Market. once you reach the topo, you will find many trails for trekking, biking or cross country skiing, a wide square that often hosts public events, a restaurant, and the Troll forest (the city's largest playground!). The view is simply spectacular.

Bryggen

Bryggen is the name of the old wharf of Bergen built by the Hanseatic merchants to store goods coming from and heading to the great North, paricularly stockfish and cereal. Severely damaged by many fires, the remaining 62 houses of the district were rebuilt in 1955. Today Bryggen is one of the 8 sites in Norway to be included in the UNESCO's World Heritage list.

Krohnegården badeplass

Just 5 minutes away from the Fyllingsdalen district, this nice bathing area on the shores of lake Storavatnet features a sandy beach, and a swimming raft. It is also accessible for visitors with mobility impairment thanks to a dedicated bathing ramp. You can reach the area by car or using one of the many paths and boulders.

Ulriken

Hiking up Mount Ulriken, the highest mount in Bergen's area, is a challenge you can't miss. There are several routes to the top of Ulriken, but if walking on hundreds of ice-covered stone steps is just not your cup of tea, you can reach it thanks to the cable car and buy yourself a hot chocolate at the bar once arrived. Be aware, though: it will swing a lot!

Fantoft Stavkirke

This beautiful wooden church was originally built in in Fortun in Sogn in 1150. To save it from demolition, consul Fredrik Georg Gade bought it and moved it in pieces to Fantoft in 1883. In the summer of 1992 it was destroyed by fire, believed to have been started by Varg Vikernes, the black metal artist founder of the one-man band Burzum.

Learn more...
Bergenhus Festning

Bergenhus Fortress is one of Norway’s oldest fortifications. From middle ages to WWII, its buildings (including Håkonshallen and Rosenkrantztårnet) had many purposes: defensive and administrative structure, royal residence, and even church. Today, Bergenhus is used as a venue for concerts and public events.

Things to know before visiting Bergen


Bargen has an efficient public transport system that links most of the city areas, including all the major points of interest. Using the app Skyss Billett you can decide to buy a single use, weekly, monthly, or seasonal ticket, depending on what you need. Tickets are valid on both buses and land rail.

There is no easy way to say it: yes it is. Every immigrant or tourist would agree that compared to prices in the EU, Norway is generally more expensive, but there are good reasons for that. Fresh fruit and vegetables are often imported from other countries, so the price should cover taxes and transport costs (I've seen strawberries from the Netherlands, zucchini from Spain, and apples from Italy). Moreover, every alchoholic beverage over 4° is under state monopoly,there are special shops where to buy it, but only during working days. The best way to save money is to avoid restaurants or tourist-traps like the central fish market, and rely instead on affordable supermarkets like REMA 1000 or Kiwi . Another good tip is to eat local products .

Let's be honest. If you expect to find the same nightlife of Madrid or Milan, you will be disappointed. Norwegians have a very different schedule from Mediterraneans, so bars and pubs close early in the evening. But maybe you are looking for the wrong things! There are tons of things you can do to fight boredom and have fun like a Norwegian. For instance, if you like hiking, climbing, trekking, biking or swimming, here you will find the playground of your dreams. If you are more of a couch potato, there are "språkkafe" events where you can test your spoken Norwegian with other learners, but also knitting clubs, art exhibitions, and every indoor activity you can imagine of.

People in Norway definitely have a sweet tooth, and waffles are one of the most loved culinary traditions of all. My best place for waffles in Bergen is unarguably BarBarista, just a couple of minutes away from the cable car station. As you walk in, the lovely couple owning the place will welcome you in a warm ad rich environment. The atmosphere inside is kaleidoscopic: sit on a couch and enjoy the incredible variety of paintings, umbrellas, toys an unusual decorations on the walls (including a full size naked portrait of the lady owner herself). Be sure to try the waffle's traditional recipe, with raspberry jam and brown cheese!
